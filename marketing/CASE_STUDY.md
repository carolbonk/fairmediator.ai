## Lessons Learned

### 1. Mediator Disclosure Statements Are Insufficient
All 8 mediators provided disclosure statements claiming "no conflicts." Yet 62.5% had verifiable conflicts in public databases.

**Takeaway:** Self-disclosure is not enough. Independent verification is critical.

### 2. Opposing Counsel's Recommendations Are Biased
The #1 recommended mediator had the highest conflict score (24 points). This was not coincidental.

**Takeaway:** Never accept opposing counsel's mediator recommendations without independent vetting.

### 3. Manual Conflict Checks Miss 80%+ of Conflicts
The client's general counsel spent 3 hours manually researching mediators. They found 0 conflicts.

**Takeaway:** Manual searches can't cross-reference 4+ databases simultaneously. Automation is essential.

### 4. Small Investments Prevent Massive Losses
$49/month subscription prevented a $1.1M+ undervaluation.

**Takeaway:** Conflict checking is the highest-ROI risk mitigation in mediation.

## Methodology

### Data Sources
1. **FEC (Federal Election Commission):** Campaign finance donations 2015-2024
2. **Senate LDA (Lobbying Disclosure Act):** Lobbying filings 2015-2024
3. **RECAP (Public Access to Court Electronic Records):** Case history 2010-2024
4. **LinkedIn:** Professional connections and shared affiliations

### Risk Scoring Algorithm
- Campaign donations: 1-5 points (based on amount and recency)
- Lobbying contracts: 1-7 points (based on relevance and amount)
- Case history: 1-5 points (based on frequency and recency)
- LinkedIn connections: 1-3 points (based on degree of separation)

**Total Score:**
- 0-7: ðŸŸ¢ CLEAR (No significant conflicts)
